History of Grappling · c. 1410

Fiore dei Liberi

Fior di Battaglia — The Flower of Battle

Getty MS Ludwig XV 13 Cividale del Friuli, Patriarchate of Aquileia 47 folios, 91 pages About
E comiçemo al braçare. L'oquale sie di dua razone: come da plazo e di ira, coe per la uita cu ogni inzano e falsira e crudelita che si po fare.
"And we begin with grappling. Which is of two kinds: one from pleasure, and one from anger — that is, for one's life, with every cunning and deception and cruelty that can be done." — Folio 2r

Written around 1410 by the Friulian knight and fencing master Fiore Furlano de'i Liberi, the Fior di Battaglia is the most complete surviving medieval Italian combat treatise. Dedicated to Niccolò III d'Este, Marquis of Ferrara, it covers unarmed grappling, dagger, sword in one and two hands, armored combat, poleaxe, spear, and mounted fighting. The Getty manuscript — the most complete of four surviving copies — contains 47 folios of illustrated instruction, each play spoken in the voice of the figure performing it.

Fiore writes in Middle Italian (Venetian dialect, c. 1410). His technical vocabulary maps directly onto modern combat sports concepts — the words encode distinctions that practitioners still navigate today.

Solaço / Ira noun pair 3 occurrences · fol. 2r, 10v
”Pleasure” vs. “Anger.” The Getty (fol. 2r) distinguishes two kinds of grappling (dua razone): da solaço — from pleasure, cooperative — vs. di ira — from anger, ”with every cunning and deception and cruelty that can be done”. The same technique belongs to both categories; only intent transforms it.
Modern parallel: The distinction between drilling/positional sparring (solaço) and full competition or self-defense (ira) is reminiscent of how combat sports gym operates in terms of intensity variations.
Sbarra / Starria noun 8 occurrences · fol. 1r, 2r
”The barriers” or “the lists.” A fenced enclosure for formal judicial combat. When Fiore says a student ”debea cobattere m[en] Starria” — had to fight in the barriers — he means a legal duel with witnesses, stakes, and rules. Armoured, with specified weapons, often before nobility. Dangerous but survivable: ”nine times out of ten nobody dies, because they are taken prisoner” (Hatcher, fol. 1v–2r).
Modern parallel: The closest analogue is MMA or professional fighting — a very violent contest that nonetheless has rules, a referee, and a cage. Fiore explicitly distinguishes this from both training (solaço) and mortal combat (ad oltrança).
Oltrança noun / adverb 2 occurrences · fol. 1r
”To the utterance” / “to the extreme.” Fighting ad oltrança means to death or surrender. Either in the barriers or in the battlefield. This is the context where ira techniques apply — strikes to the eyes, nose, temples, chin, and flanks (Getty fol. 2r). Fiore teaches both tiers in the same system; the student must know which techniques belong to which context.
Modern parallel: Self-defense vs. sport — the techniques that are ”too dangerous to practice with courtesy” (Hatcher, PD carta 2a) are the ones illegal in competition. Fiore documents them alongside the legal ones and trusts the student to know the difference.
Çogho / Zogho noun 57 occurrences · 26 folios
“Play” or “game.” Every technique is a zogho. Not metaphorical — the same word means both a game and a combat technique. Zogho largo = wide play (long range). Zogho stretto = narrow play (close range, grappling distance). The word itself encodes the training/fighting ambiguity: every “game” is also a “fight,” and every “fight” is also a “game.”
Modern parallel: “Rolling” in BJJ, “randori” in judo, your "A-Game", etc.  The ludic framing is ancient.
Zugadore / Çugadore noun 26 occurrences · 9 folios
“Player.” The person being thrown or controlled in a technique demonstration. Fiore’s deliberate choice over “enemy” or “adversary” — the zugadore is the one who attacks so the master or scholar can demonstrate the response. In illustrations, the player wears no insignia (no crown, no garter).
Modern parallel: Uke in judo/aikido — the training partner who feeds the attack. Fiore’s choice of “player” over “attacker” reveals a system designed around cooperative practice, even when documenting lethal techniques.
Prese noun, plural 59 occurrences · 25 folios
”Holds” or “grips.” The atomic unit of abrazare. The Getty (fol. 2r) frames holds within the solaço/ira distinction — holds practiced in pleasure vs. holds for real combat. The Pisani Dossi (carta 2a) adds that certain holds are ”prese e zoghi che non se po çugar de cortesia” — holds and plays that cannot be played with courtesy. Separately, at the grappling guards opening, Hatcher renders: ”Holds are not superior unless they give you an advantage” (fol. 6r).
Modern parallel: Grips in wrestling, judo, BJJ. The concept that a grip is not just contact but control with intent is universal across grappling traditions.
Posta noun 269 occurrences · 61 folios
”Guard” or “position.” Literally “post” or “station” — where you stand and wait. Every weapon section opens with guards. In grappling: Posta Longa (Long Guard), Dente di Cengiaro (Boar’s Tusk), Porta di Ferro (Iron Gate), Posta Frontale (Forehead Guard). Each guard is a persona that speaks in first person, declaring its nature. The most frequent technical term in the manuscript.
Modern parallel: Stance in wrestling/MMA, posture in BJJ. The concept of named positions as starting points for all technique has never left grappling.
Ligadure / Roture noun pair 19 occurrences · 8 folios
”Binds” and “breaks.” The two outcomes of a successful hold. Ligadure = locks, joint control (binding the arm so “he has no more defense nor can free himself”, Getty fol. 4r). Roture = breaks, dislocations (”breaking arms and legs”, Getty fol. 4r). Listed as two of the eight attributes of grappling. The distinction is control vs. destruction.
Modern parallel: Submissions in BJJ split along the same line — you can control (pin, hold) or finish (break, choke). The ligadure/roture pair maps to the tap-or-snap decision.
Remedio / Contrario noun pair 102 occurrences · 43 folios
“Remedy” and “Counter.” The pedagogical chain that structures the entire book. The Magistro Remedio (Remedy Master) shows a defense. His scholars demonstrate variations. The Magistro Contrario (Counter Master) defeats the remedy. Occasionally a fourth master (Contra-Contrario) defeats the counter. Three-deep, sometimes four. Each wears insignia: crowns for masters, garters for scholars.
Modern parallel: The technique → counter → re-counter chain is the backbone of every grappling curriculum. Fiore formalised it with a visual system (crowns and garters) six centuries before modern instructionals.
Armiçare verb / noun 5 occurrences · fol. 1r, 2r, 4r
”The art of armed fighting” / “to fight with arms.” Fiore’s name for his complete system: L’Arte d’Armiçare or La Scientia d’Armiçare. Notably, arte may mean simply “skill” and scientia “knowledge” — “the skill and knowledge of fighting.” He explicitly compares it to law and medicine: ”If I had studied forty years in civil law… I would be a doctor in those three sciences” (Hatcher, fol. 2r).
Modern parallel: The claim that martial arts is an intellectual discipline requiring systematic study and documentation — not just physical talent — is Fiore’s most modern argument. It anticipates every debate about “is fighting an art or a sport?”
Abrazare verb / noun 11 occurrences · fol. 2r, 4r
“To grapple” / “grappling.” Literally “to embrace.” The first section of the treatise and the foundation of the whole system. Fiore lists eight attributes: strength, speed, advantageous holds, breaks, binds, strikes to vulnerable points, throws, and dislocations. The Pisani Dossi lists seven (combining the last two). This is the section where the solaço/ira distinction is introduced.
Modern parallel: The word itself — “embrace” — captures the intimacy of grappling that distinguishes it from striking. Fiore begins here, before weapons, because grappling is where all armed combat ends up at close range.

The manuscript operates in six distinct rhetorical modes, often layered within a single passage. This is not a manual in the modern sense — it is a dramatic performance on parchment, where each figure speaks in first person, boasting, threatening, and instructing simultaneously.

Conditional Tactics 98 passages
Densest in Grappling, Dagger, Sword
”And if that lock looks like it will fail me, then I will switch to one of the other locks that follow.”
The “if… then… alternatively” structure maps directly to how modern coaches teach: primary technique → read the reaction → follow-up. Evidence that Fiore’s abrazare is a flowchart-based system, not a technique catalogue.
Threat to Opponent 94 passages
Everywhere — densest in Grappling + Dagger
”When I press my thumb under your ear you will feel so much pain that you will go to the ground for sure.”
The violence is the specification. “Force you to the ground” is the functional description; “pain and suffering” is the consequence that motivates learning both the technique and its counter.
First-Person Boast 60 passages
Guards, Seven Swords, section openers
”I am the Iron Gate, full of danger. Those who oppose me will always end up in pain and suffering.”
Each guard is a persona with a voice. The boast is the mnemonic — you remember what the Iron Gate does because the Iron Gate tells you. Persona-based pedagogy.
Counter-Rhetoric 50 passages
Dagger, Mounted — “I make the counter to the play before me”
”What you plan to do cannot always be done.”
The counter always references the previous technique explicitly, creating a linked-list structure. Each counter acknowledges the validity of what it defeats — not dismissal but dialectic. The system teaches through opposition.
Philosophical 48 passages
Preface almost exclusively
”This art is so vast that there is no man in the world with so great a memory that he could keep in mind without books a quarter of this art.”
Fiore claims intellectual parity with law and medicine. The solaço/ira passage isn’t just a preface remark — it’s the emotional architecture of the whole manuscript. Guards are theatrical (solaço), grappling plays are violent (ira), counters are dialectical.
Autobiographical 33 passages
Preface only (fol. 1r–2r), then Fiore disappears
”Five times, for my honor, I was compelled to fight in strange places, without relatives and without friends, having no hope except in God, in my art, and in my sword.”
Fiore front-loads all personal history then vanishes. The figures take over. The separation is deliberate: author-as-credentialed-expert (preface) vs. system-as-self-demonstrating (body). He fought five duels because envious masters challenged him — he won all five, unarmoured, with sharp swords.

Fiore's contexts of combat. The Getty (fol. 2r) names two kinds: solaço (pleasure — cooperative practice) and ira (anger — for one's life). Separately, in fol. 1r–1v, he discusses sbarra (the barriers — formal armoured combat, where ”it is rare that anyone dies”) and contrasts it with unarmoured duelling with sharp swords, where ”a single mistake in your defense could cost you your life.” Critically, the barriers can themselves be a oltrança — to the death. So armour and rules don't eliminate lethality; they reduce it. Fiore teaches one system across all these contexts. When envious masters forced him to fight five times — unarmoured, with sharp swords, in strange places — he proved it worked.

The Pisani Dossi MS (dated February 10, 1409) is a shorter, more condensed redaction by the same author, Fiore dei Liberi. Dedicated to Niccolò III d'Este. 36 folios vs. the Getty's 47. The images below are from Novati's 1902 facsimile. Where the Getty uses solaço ("pleasure") for cooperative grappling, the Pisani Dossi uses d'amore ("of love") — a different word choice that may reflect evolving thought across the decade separating the manuscripts.
