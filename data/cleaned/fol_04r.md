## Translation

[continuing the eight things wrestling requires:] ...that is knowing quick advantages, knowing how to make breaks -- that is breaking arms and legs, knowing locks -- that is binding the arms so that a man has no more defence nor can free himself. Knowing how to strike to the most dangerous places.

Also knowing how to throw someone to the ground without danger to oneself.

Also knowing how to dislocate arms and legs in various ways.

And all of which things will be found and may be depicted in this book, step by step as the art requires.

We have said what wrestling requires. Now let us speak of the guards of wrestling.

The guards of wrestling can be made in various ways. And one way is better than another.

But these four guards are the best in armour and without armour. Although the guards do not have stability because of the sudden holds that are made.

The first four masters that you see with crowns on their heads -- through them are shown the guards of wrestling. That is: Posta Longa [Long Position], and Dente di Cenghiaro [Boar's Tusk], which work one against the other. And then Porta di Ferro [Iron Gate] and Posta Frontale [Frontal Position], one against the other.

And these four guards can do all the things said before about wrestling in armour and without armour. That is: holds and locks and breaks.

Now it is necessary to explain how the guards are distinguished from the masters, the players, the scholars from the players, the players from the masters, and the remedy from the counter -- although the counter is always placed after the remedy. And sometimes the remedy after, or after all its plays, and of this we shall make clear.

We say that to observe the guards or positions is a light thing, since the guards have their weapons in hand one against the other, and they do not touch one another.

And they stand watchful and firm one against the other to see what the companion will do.

These are called positions or guards or first masters of the battle.

And they wear a crown on their head, because they are placed in position and in a way to make great defence with such a posture.

And they are the beginning of that art -- that is, of the art of the weapon with which the said masters stand in guard.

And "position" means the same as "guard."

And "guard" means that a man guards himself, and defends himself with it, from the blows of his enemy.

And "position" means the way to await his enemy and not to attack him without danger to himself.

The other master who follows the four guards comes to attack from the guards, and comes to defend himself from another player with the blows that issue from the four guards that come before. This master also wears a crown, and is called the second master.

He is also called the remedy master, because he makes the remedy so that blows are not given to him, or that no injury is done to him in that art

[continuing in right column:] ...which are the said positions or guards.

And this second, that is the remedy, has several players under him who play those plays that the master before them -- that is the remedy -- could play, taking that cover or hold that the said remedy makes. And these players will wear a device at the knee. And these players will make all the plays of the remedy until there is found another master who will make the counter to the remedy and to all his players.

And because he acts against the remedy and against its players, he will wear the device of the remedy master and his players -- that is the crown on his head and the device below the knee.

And this one is called the third master, and is called the counter, and he will do this for the other masters and in similar plays.

Furthermore I say that in some places in the art there is found a fourth master -- that is one who acts against the third, that is the counter to the remedy. And this is the fourth master, called master four.

And he is called the counter-counter.

Although few plays pass the third master in the art.

And if more are made, they would be dangerous. And enough has been said of these things.

As we have spoken here before of the guards of wrestling, and of the second master -- that is of the remedy and his players, and of the third master who acts against the second master and his players, and of the fourth master who is called counter-counter -- just as these masters and players govern the art of wrestling in armour and without armour, so do these masters and players govern the art of the lance and the lances, and their guards, masters and players.

And likewise with the axe, and with the sword in one hand and in two hands. And likewise with the dagger.

So that in effect these masters and players mentioned above, with their insignia and devices, govern all the art of arms on foot and on horseback, in armour and without armour.

According as they do in the play of wrestling.

And this is to be understood solely because it is necessary that there be guards and masters in the other arts and in the remedies and counters as in the art of wrestling, so that the book may be clearly understood.

Although the figures written below, and the plays, will show all the art, so well that all of it may be understood.

And we attend to the painted figures and to their plays and to their words, which show us the truth.

## Notes
- This folio completes the prologue/introduction of the treatise. The folio number "4" is visible in the upper right corner.
- The eight attributes of wrestling (begun at the end of fol. 2r) are completed here: (1) strength, (2) speed, (3) knowledge of quick advantages, (4) knowing how to break arms/legs, (5) knowing locks, (6) knowing how to strike dangerous places, (7) knowing how to throw without danger to oneself, (8) knowing how to dislocate arms/legs.
- The pedagogical system is fully explained: First Master (guards, wears crown) -> Second Master / Remedy (defences, wears crown) -> Scholars/Players (plays of the remedy, wear garter at knee) -> Third Master / Counter (counters the remedy, wears crown + garter) -> Fourth Master / Counter-Counter (rare).
- The four guards of wrestling named: Posta Longa, Dente di Cenghiaro, Porta di Ferro, Posta Frontale.
- The closing line -- "E nui attendemo ale figure depinte e a lor çoghi e a loro parole le quale ne mostrana la uerita" -- transitions from prologue to the illustrated portion of the manuscript.
- Corrections from existing OCR: "ayagro/ayagri" consistently normalized to "magistro/magistri" (the OCR had many garbled readings of this word); various other abbreviations expanded more consistently.
