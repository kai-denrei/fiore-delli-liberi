## Translation

Top-left:
I am the first master and called the remedy because armed I go and with valor I lead, so that he cannot do [harm] to me. And his strikes and flower-plays, my covers will not fail. And this my method is not for [mere] flower-play. My dagger makes flower-covers. Raising my cover and this finisher.

Top-right:
And with my dagger I come back into an arm's [length] hold, my release. And that you watch the guard, with the [?] do not fail to stop. The [?] to me of the flower-plays without dagger in my flower-play. And the drops [of blood?].

Bottom-left:
He is the counter and defender. Of short covers and royal flower-plays. From where [?] not by flower-play of weakness. In rule and in release, my flower and power of strength. And that the fixings are not in the fortress.

Bottom-right:
The counter of the cover that my flower-play [makes]. The [?] my quick seeing at that place where the prey [is]. And with that at the arms. And striking down in the true wrath.

## Notes
Four scenes in a 2x2 grid. All are dagger combat. Top-left: Two figures facing each other, one holding a dagger overhead (the "primo magistro" / first master called "remedie" / the remedy), the other approaching with a dagger thrust. Top-right: A crowned figure with a dagger facing an opponent — appears to be a close-quarters exchange. Bottom-left: Two figures, one appears to be a woman in a dress/long garments (or a robed figure) facing a dagger-wielding opponent — possibly depicting a counter or defensive technique. Bottom-right: Two figures in a clinch with daggers, one attempting to strike while the other controls. The text identifies the top-left master as "remedie" (the remedy), a key concept in Fiore's system — the master who provides the defensive counter to an attack. Text continues to be very challenging to read.
