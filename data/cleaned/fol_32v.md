## Translation

Top-left:
For the art of the Flower. Two Masters. That the first one well demonstrates. He who thrusts here but for those ceremonies of the art. And the purchase itself. That they are the guard for the brief grace. The Superior. But make the art. For the quick short. The Superior. The art comes to the purchase. And to one guard then here it appears to be the counter.

**Guard: Short Serpentine (Posta breve la Serpentina).**

Top-right:
Guard of the Cross. That my contrary. The contrary feints. That for the guard against him. From my preparation and the large guard of the art, I strike. Broken from the art's strike. The contrary that has the failing. The conception. That for her. And the verse. I have and I am the art. The error — not this, to us failure.

**Guard: Of the Cross (Posta di una croce).**

Bottom-left:
Simonetta and the question. For the Superior [guard]. In the manner of the forenamed guard, how strong being that the art was certain. Coming to the guard. True art — guard against the strike. And who the contrary is apt to the art.

**Guard: Serpentine the Superior (Dente serpentina la Soperana).**

Bottom-right:
Guard of the Iron at the Middle. That called. And of the art split that is to this just art. The posture of the art. And giving to the guard. The middle of the coming short. And named art they can guard. And remaining.

**Guard: Iron at the Middle (Posta de ferre la meçana).**

## Notes
This page begins the section on the guards (poste) of the sword in armor (spada in arme). Four figures are shown, each in a named guard position. Each figure wears a crown (indicating master status). The guard names appear in red ink below each figure:
- Top-left: Posta breve la Serpentina (Short Serpentine Guard)
- Top-right: Posta di una croce (Guard of the Cross)
- Bottom-left: Dente serpentina la Soperana (Serpentine Tooth the Superior)
- Bottom-right: Posta de ferre la meçana (Middle Iron Gate Guard)
These are the opening guards for armored sword combat. Text is written in red and brown ink.
