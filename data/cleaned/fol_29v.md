## Translation

Top-left:
Of the counter of the Scholar from my Scholar in the forehand cover that is your guard. The turning half-blow in the thick. Well I show you that we do no less.

Top-right:
In this play I draw from the play of the short. For the great remedy of the art. That you extend to the manner, making the arm-lock turning. And reversed. The art that greatly guards the manner. Rise from the blow. Stop the blow.
Of the counter-play that from the short. The forehand cover of the brave short barbarian. That I have well from two, this art.

Bottom-left:
I make the contrary — who acts against the scholar that I counter the forehand, that was covered in time. For that which is to me the grip, making the play in this from the ground. This is well a remedy indeed. For all of the grip.

Bottom-right:
For the firm counter. We of the player together in pair. In the sixth. The reason the wise one makes to enter this play, to make secure. That also the play of which then this. Against the quick barbarian. Against such, this art.

## Notes
Four combat pairs continuing the two-handed sword section. The bottom-left illustration shows one combatant being thrown to the ground (a wrestling/grappling technique from the sword). The bottom-right shows close-quarters grappling with swords. Text is heavily abbreviated and difficult to read; several readings are approximate. The script shows typical features of the Venetian chancery hand.
