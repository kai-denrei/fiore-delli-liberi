# Grappling Section QA — OCR vs Hatcher/PDF

Comparison of our Claude Vision OCR transcriptions/translations against the authoritative
Chidester/Hatcher translations from the Wiktenauer PDF compilation.

Getty MS (Ludwig XV 13), folios 06r through 08r.

---

## Folio 06r — The Four Grappling Guards

### 6r-a: The Long Guard (Posta Longa)

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> I am the Long Guard and I seek you like this. And in response to the first grapple that you attempt on me I will bring my right arm up under your left arm. And I will then execute the first play of the Abrazare. And with that lock I will force you to the ground. And if that lock looks like it will fail me, then I will switch to one of the other locks that follow.

**Claude (OCR):**
> Here I stand in the long guard and here I wait for you. And in the position that bears [the gate]. My left arm reaches forward. And on my right flank I have the certainty of putting you into whatever hold pleases me most. And from the hold I will also act. And that position [has its] completion and response. The tree [of combat] that every player understands.

**Discrepancies:**
- MAJOR: OCR says "left arm reaches forward" — Hatcher says "right arm up under your left arm." The arm references are confused.
- MAJOR: OCR misses the core tactical instruction entirely: bringing the arm under the opponent's arm to execute the first play. Instead it reads as a vague description.
- MAJOR: OCR adds "The tree [of combat] that every player understands" — not present in Hatcher. This may be a misreading of marginal text or a gloss.
- The OCR reads "Aqui posta longa" (Here is the long guard) vs the Latin/Italian that Hatcher renders as "I am the Long Guard."
- OCR loses the conditional logic: "if that lock looks like it will fail me, then I will switch" — this tactical branching is absent.

### 6r-b: The Boar's Tusk (Dente di Zenghiar)

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> I counter you with the Boar's Tusk. And with this move I am sure to break your grip. And from this guard I can transition to the Iron Gate, which will force you to the ground. And if my plan fails me because of your defense, I will seek other ways to hurt you, for example with breaks, binds and dislocations, as you see depicted in these drawings.

**Claude (OCR):**
> It is for me to play against you and to break my guard like a wedge. Always in another [position] I defend proudly. I will throw you to the ground and [?]. And that one says more to me with a counter [?]. And who is also bare [unarmed] descends from the gate and against. Who finds themselves in a guard of displacement. In that change that is needed for the play.

**Discrepancies:**
- MAJOR: OCR completely misses the guard name "Boar's Tusk" (Dente di Zenghiar). Reads "like a wedge" (cugno) but misses the identity.
- MAJOR: "break your grip" (Hatcher) becomes "break my guard" (OCR) — opposite meaning.
- MAJOR: The transition to Iron Gate is absent in the OCR.
- MAJOR: The specific techniques list — "breaks, binds and dislocations" — is entirely absent.
- OCR produces largely incoherent text for the second half of this passage.
- The OCR transcription itself shows problems: "A mi e de Zughare contra e ce acogno" should likely be "In dente di zenghiar contra ti io vegno" (as in the critical Italian).

### 6r-c: The Iron Gate (Porta di Ferro)

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> I wait for you without moving in the Iron Gate, ready to grapple with all of my skill. And this guard can be applied not only in the art of grappling, but also in the art of the Spear, the Poleax, the Sword, and the Dagger. For I am the Iron Gate, full of danger. Those who oppose me will always end up in pain and suffering. And as for those of you who come against me trying to get your hands on me, I will force you to the ground.

**Claude (OCR):**
> In the position of the tooth I wait for you without much [hesitation] and I seize the quick, tight hold of my position. The hump of [that] strike is evil and bitter. For my arms are quick and strike with great force. Who comes against me is stunned from afar. And who comes against me with their own counter-hold. And the proper holds are to make the completion.

**Discrepancies:**
- MAJOR: OCR identifies this as "position of the tooth" — but this is the IRON GATE (Porta di Ferro), not Dente (Tooth). Guard names are confused.
- MAJOR: The entire passage about applicability across weapon arts (Spear, Poleax, Sword, Dagger) is completely absent in the OCR.
- MAJOR: "I am the Iron Gate, full of danger" — this key self-identification is missing.
- The OCR seems to have conflated content from this panel with another, possibly reading parts of the adjacent text.
- OCR adds material about "arms are quick and strike with great force" not in Hatcher.

### 6r-d: The Guard of the Forehead/Headband (Posta Frontale)

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> I am the Guard of the Forehead, used to get my hands on you. Now if I come against you in this guard, you may lay hands on me. But I will then move from this guard, and with skill I will take you down to the Iron Gate. Then I will make you suffer as if you had fallen into the depths of hell. And I will serve you so effectively with locks and dislocations, that you will quickly acknowledge my superiority. And as long as I don't forget my skills, I will gain my superior holds.

**Claude (OCR):**
> I am the Frontal [position] and the proper counter-hold. For in this position I can best make myself ready, that is in the lesser of this hold. Two binds and the greater holds of the tooth and against the strong one who punishes the weak. To flee and to recover and to make a good counter. And through the embrace against the counter-hold. And the counter-holds I will make finely.

**Discrepancies:**
- PARTIAL MATCH: Both identify this as "Frontal" / "Guard of the Forehead" — the only guard correctly identified.
- MAJOR: The tactical sequence — transitioning to Iron Gate, making opponent suffer "as if fallen into hell" — is entirely absent in OCR.
- MAJOR: "locks and dislocations" (Hatcher) becomes vague "two binds and the greater holds" (OCR).
- OCR loses the dramatic rhetorical quality of the original.
- OCR adds "the lesser of this hold" and "the tooth" references not in Hatcher.

## Folio 06v — First Through Fourth Plays of Grappling

### 6v-a: First Play of Grappling

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> This is the first play of grappling and from every grappling guard you can arrive at this play, and from this position, proceed as follows: jam his right inside elbow with your left hand, and bring your right hand up behind and against his left elbow as shown. Now quickly make the second play, that is to say, having gripped him like this, turn your body to the left, and as a result he either goes to the ground or his arm will be dislocated.

**Claude (OCR):**
> This is the first hold of wrestling. And here the cheek [grip]. It is a belt hold and counter-bind and proper twist. For my right hand goes under the left hand of the scholar. She [the technique] makes me do the frontal bind. And to break [?] the hold of the pledge is above for gripping and revenge. Seize the collar at the waist. And through this play break it. Well with strength, against the counter for the good player.

**Discrepancies:**
- PARTIAL MATCH: Both identify this as the first play of grappling/wrestling.
- MAJOR: The specific hand placement — "jam his right inside elbow with your left hand, and bring your right hand up behind and against his left elbow" — is garbled in the OCR. OCR says "right hand goes under the left hand of the scholar" which reverses the action.
- MAJOR: "turn your body to the left, and as a result he either goes to the ground or his arm will be dislocated" — this key instruction is absent.
- OCR introduces "cheek [grip]," "belt hold," "frontal bind" — terms not in Hatcher's rendering.
- OCR reads "guancha" as cheek — may be a misreading of the manuscript.

### 6v-b: Second Scholar

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> As the Scholar of the First Master says, I am certain to put this man to the ground, either by breaking or dislocating his left arm. And if the player who fights with the First Master takes his left hand off the shoulder of the Remedy Master in order to make a defense, then I will quickly let go of his right arm with my left hand and instead seize his left leg with my left hand, and grip his throat with my right hand in order to throw him to the ground, as you see depicted in the third play.

**Claude (OCR):**
> The Scholar of the first master. And who by the [?] proper to him. Accompanied, who is faithful and of the [?]. The hand guards and in the chain of the player's hold. A good and straight serpentine guard [?] against the established position. The short hold.

**Discrepancies:**
- MAJOR: OCR captures almost none of the tactical content. The entire conditional sequence — if the player removes his hand from the shoulder, then transition to seizing the leg and gripping the throat — is completely absent.
- MAJOR: "breaking or dislocating his left arm" is absent.
- OCR introduces "serpentine guard" — a term not in Hatcher and likely a misreading.
- The OCR transcription is largely incoherent for this panel, suggesting severe reading difficulties.

### 6v-c: Third Scholar

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> The scholar that came before me speaks truly that from his hold he will force his opponent to the ground or dislocate his left arm. As he told you, if the player takes away his left hand from the shoulder of the Remedy Master, then the Remedy Master transitions to the Third Play, as you see depicted here. Thus, the First play and the Second play are really one single play, where the Remedy Master forces the player to the ground with a turn of his body, while in this Third play the player is thrown to the ground onto his back.

**Claude (OCR):**
> This Scholar demonstrates that I can well place my hand in the hold of the scholar. And the counter is [controlled] in my cover. Preparing well and the Scholar. For whoever rages, the players place the hand of the Scholar in the hold of the master under the opponent. The counter placed well above. The Scholar knows the cloth of this [technique]. That the hold separates and the Scholar [acts] well. To measure the change. In the [?] a rod. I seize the left at the shoulder to reverse.

**Discrepancies:**
- MAJOR: The structural relationship between plays 1, 2, and 3 — that the first two are really one play and the third is the alternative — is completely absent.
- MAJOR: "Remedy Master" concept is absent; OCR does not use this important term.
- MAJOR: The key technique — throwing the player onto his back — is absent.
- OCR produces a largely incoherent text with phantom terms like "cloth of this technique."
- The only partial connection: OCR mentions "shoulder" and "seize the left" which vaguely echoes the content.

### 6v-d: Fourth Play of Grappling

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> This is the Fourth Play of grappling, by which the Student can easily force the player to the ground. And if he cannot force him to ground like this, he will seek other plays and techniques and use other methods, as you will see depicted below. You should know that the plays and the techniques will not always work in every situation, so if you do not have a good hold, you should quickly seek one, so as not to let your opponent gain any advantage over you.

**Claude (OCR):**
> And this is the fourth coupling of the embrace that the third [teaches] me. The Scholar goes to the [positions] and I will do [?]. The encounter. And also to operate. That this [?] of the Scholar does not belt [?]. That position well carried. And turns to the held turn. And this is the embrace. And whoever loses the foot. And the attachment goes [?] to the hand and then. [?].

**Discrepancies:**
- PARTIAL MATCH: Both identify this as the fourth play.
- MAJOR: The important pedagogical principle — "plays and techniques will not always work in every situation, so if you do not have a good hold, you should quickly seek one" — is absent.
- MAJOR: The strategic advice about not letting the opponent gain advantage is absent.
- OCR is largely incoherent for the second half of this passage.

## Folio 07r — Fifth Through Eighth Scholars

### 7r-a: Fifth Scholar

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> This grip that I make with my right hand at your throat will bring you pain and suffering, and with it I will force you to the ground. Also let me tell you that if I seize you under your left knee with my right hand, I will be even more certain of driving you into the ground.

**Claude (OCR):**
> This hold where my right hand is at the head of the scholar. I can then push and grip. And this is the stable turn. Also I need [to place] my left hand, under his right [arm], and I can then make the circle to throw him to the ground.

**Discrepancies:**
- PARTIAL MATCH: Both describe a right-hand grip and throwing to ground.
- SIGNIFICANT: Hatcher says "right hand at your throat" — OCR says "right hand at the head." Throat vs head is a meaningful difference for technique reconstruction.
- SIGNIFICANT: Hatcher's alternative — seizing under the left knee — is absent in OCR. Instead OCR describes placing the left hand under the right arm.
- OCR reads "stable turn" which may be a misreading of "volta stabile."

### 7r-b: Counter to the Fifth Scholar

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> I am the counter of the Fifth Play that is shown earlier. And let me explain that if with my right hand I push up the elbow of his hand that seeks to harm me, I will turn him in such a way that either I will force him to the ground, as you see here depicted, or I will gain a hold or a lock, and so I will have little concern for his grappling skills.

**Claude (OCR):**
> I am his counter. Because of the hand in position, I take from him and from the hand under the arm and the arms are locked or held. And I send [him] stable. And I change to the nature of retreat. And I change short so that he cannot [resist]. And from me the embrace makes a certain hold.

**Discrepancies:**
- PARTIAL MATCH: Both identify this as a counter.
- SIGNIFICANT: The specific technique — "push up the elbow" — is absent; OCR has a vague "hand under the arm."
- The OCR captures the general idea of gaining a hold/lock but loses the specificity.
- "I will have little concern for his grappling skills" — this rhetorical flourish is absent.

### 7r-c: Sixth Scholar

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> From this hold that I have gained, and by the way I hold you, I will lift you off the ground with my strength and throw you down under my feet head first with your body following. And as far as I am concerned, you will not be able to counter me.

**Claude (OCR):**
> For the hold called the counter-four. In the way that I make him turn. I seize the arms and my strength. And from the point. I take the full cover. The one above who pretends. Of the beak [?]. And the counter alone does not flourish against the counter.

**Discrepancies:**
- MAJOR: The dramatic technique — lifting opponent off the ground and throwing head-first — is completely absent in OCR.
- MAJOR: "you will not be able to counter me" vs OCR's confused "the counter alone does not flourish against the counter."
- OCR introduces "counter-four" and "beak" — terms not in Hatcher.
- OCR is largely incoherent for this passage.

### 7r-d: Eighth Scholar (Thumb Under Ear)

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> When I press my thumb under your ear you will feel so much pain that you will go to the ground for sure, or I will make other hold or lock that will be worse than torture for you...

> [Counter:] The counter that can be made is the Sixth play made against the Fifth Play, when he puts his hand underneath his opponent's elbow. This counter can certainly be done to me here.

**Claude (OCR):**
> With pain you could hold firm your cover. For I wait against you and this one goes in a manner against. Hold the Scholar. And the counter other one [?] I will make the bindings from your [strikes] more times. Which return. And which [?] can be stable. The one who covers those who change. The lock upon them. That through half with the iron [dagger?] holder also.

**Discrepancies:**
- MAJOR: The specific technique — "press my thumb under your ear" — is completely absent.
- MAJOR: The counter description (Sixth play against Fifth) is absent.
- OCR is largely incoherent. The mention of "iron [dagger?]" suggests misreading.
- Only the vaguest sense of "pain" and "lock" is preserved.

## Folio 07v — Seventh, Ninth, Tenth, and Thirteenth Scholars

### 7v-a: Seventh Scholar (Seized from Behind)

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> You seized me from behind in order to throw me to the ground, and I turned like this. And if I fail to throw you to the ground you will have a lucky escape. This play is a good finishing move, but unless this is done quickly, this remedy will fail.

**Claude (OCR):**
> You seized me from behind and my hands are raised. And so I change to the stronger [position]. For I can [?] stand against my good grip. And from this play it was the parting. That those who wish to make it stand, those who lose fail more.

**Discrepancies:**
- GOOD MATCH: Both correctly identify being seized from behind.
- SIGNIFICANT: Hatcher's "I turned like this" (the turning escape) is rendered as "I change to the stronger position" — similar but less specific.
- SIGNIFICANT: "This play is a good finishing move, but unless done quickly, this remedy will fail" — the speed requirement is absent; OCR has a vague "those who lose fail more."
- This is one of the better OCR results, capturing the general scenario correctly.

### 7v-b: Ninth Scholar (Gambarola / Leg Hook)

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> This is a play that involves a throw over the leg [the Gambarola] which is a risky move in Abrazare. So if you want to make this leg throw successfully, you will need to do it with power and speed.

**Claude (OCR):**
> This is a play of the leg-hook (gambarola) that I know well how to execute and bring to its end. And no one can tell the pain and the [?]. Then the flower [technique] is proper in the run.

**Discrepancies:**
- GOOD MATCH: Both correctly identify the Gambarola / leg hook.
- SIGNIFICANT: Hatcher emphasizes it is "risky" and needs "power and speed" — OCR misses the risk warning and instead says "I know well how to execute."
- OCR adds "no one can tell the pain" — not in Hatcher.
- "The flower [technique]" may be a misreading of "fiore" appearing in the text.

### 7v-c: Thirteenth Scholar (The Outsider)

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> This is a finishing move and it is a good way to hold someone, because they cannot defend themselves. For the counter, the one who is being held should move as quickly as he can over to a wall or a post and drive himself backwards against it so that the man holding him breaks his head or his back against the aforementioned wall or post.

**Claude (OCR):**
> This is a play. And with a proper arm-lock (Braxolna), the one on the ground [?] and the one who counters. And that one who seizes places [the grip] at the head and through his cover and beyond the punching. And this is the change of the [technique] above only, that those who resist scrape. The cover of this figure and the raised hands run [?].

**Discrepancies:**
- MAJOR: The dramatic counter — driving backward into a wall or post to break the holder's head or back — is completely absent.
- MAJOR: OCR introduces "Braxolna" (arm-lock) — not in Hatcher for this passage. This term may belong to a different panel.
- The OCR may have confused this panel with the adjacent one (the bottom-left text on 7v).
- SIGNIFICANT: "finishing move" and "they cannot defend themselves" — not captured in OCR.

### 7v-d: Tenth Scholar (Knee to Groin)

**Hatcher (PDF):**
> This student strikes his opponent with a knee to the groin to gain advantage in order to throw him to the ground. To make the counter, when your opponent comes in quickly to strike you in the groin with his knee, seize his right leg under the knee with your right hand, and throw him to the ground.

**Claude (OCR):**
> From this I will make the hip-throws of the fourth downward, growing and greater. More attached to the bindings in the line. The flourishing that is [?] that the hip-throw comes from the leg and flourishing in [?] another end of the pain and the sea [?]. Touch the maker of the force when you suffer the throws. **Discrepancies:**
- MAJOR: "knee to the groin" — this specific, important technique is completely absent in OCR.
- MAJOR: The counter — seizing the right leg under the knee — is absent.
- OCR reads "hip-throws" which is a different technique than what Hatcher describes (knee strike, not hip throw).

## Summary Assessment

### Overall Quality Rating: POOR — Not usable as-is for scholarship

### Key Findings:

1. **Guard names**: Only 1 of 4 guards correctly identified (Posta Frontale). The OCR misses Posta Longa's identity, fails to name Dente di Zenghiar entirely, and confuses Porta di Ferro with Dente.

2. **Technique descriptions**: The specific hand placements, body mechanics, and tactical instructions that make Fiore's text valuable for martial arts reconstruction are almost universally lost or garbled.

3. **Conditional logic**: Fiore frequently uses if/then structures ("if this fails, do that instead"). These are consistently absent from the OCR translations.

4. **Best passages**: 7v-a (seized from behind) and 7v-b (gambarola) are the closest to correct, capturing the general scenario and key terminology.

5. **Worst passages**: 6v-b, 6v-c, 7r-d, 7v-d, and 8r-d are largely incoherent in the OCR, bearing almost no resemblance to the Hatcher translations.

6. **Root cause**: The Italian transcriptions themselves are deeply flawed (many [?] markers, phantom words, incoherent sentences), which means the translations built on them cannot be accurate. The manuscript hand is notoriously difficult, and Claude Vision OCR struggles with 15th-century Italian cursive.

### Recommendation:

For the history of grappling page, **use the Hatcher/Chidester translations directly** rather than the OCR output. The OCR transcriptions could be retained as a starting point for future paleographic work, but they should not be presented as reliable translations. If original transcription is needed, it should be done by a specialist in 15th-century Italian hands, or the OCR should be corrected against the Hatcher text panel by panel.
