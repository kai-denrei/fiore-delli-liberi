## The Voice of the Text

Fiore's manuscript operates in at least six distinct rhetorical modes, often layered within a single passage. The text is not a manual in the modern sense — it is a **dramatic performance on parchment**, where each figure speaks in first person, boasting, threatening, and instructing simultaneously.

---

## 1. First-Person Boast (60 passages)

**Where:** Concentrated in weapon guards (Seven Swords, Sword Guards, Axe) and section-opening master figures.

Each guard and master figure speaks as a persona, declaring its identity and supremacy:

> *"I am the Iron Gate, full of danger. Those who oppose me will always end up in pain and suffering."* — fol. 6r

> *"I am the Tiger, and I am so quick to run and turn, that even the thunderbolt from heaven cannot catch me."* — fol. 32r (Segno)

> *"I am the Elephant and I carry a castle in my care, and I neither fall to my knees nor lose my footing."* — fol. 32r (Segno)

This is not idle bragging — it's **mnemonic pedagogy**. Each guard's personality encodes its tactical character. The student remembers *who* the Iron Gate is (dangerous, immovable, multi-weapon) because the figure *tells you* in its own voice. The boast is the lesson.

**Distribution:** Most concentrated in the **Segno page** (32r) and at the start of each weapon section, where guard-masters introduce themselves. Thins out in the play-by-play sections where scholars do the talking.

---

## 2. Threat to Opponent (94 passages)

**Where:** Everywhere, but densest in Grappling (12), Dagger 1st Master (14), Mounted Fencing (11).

The second most common mode. Figures address their opponent directly:

> *"This grip that I make with my right hand at your throat will bring you pain and suffering, and with it I will force you to the ground."* — fol. 7r

> *"You will go to the ground because of your lack of knowledge, and in armor this is a particularly safe throw."* — Dagger, 3rd Master

> *"When I press my thumb under your ear you will feel so much pain that you will go to the ground for sure, or I will make other hold or lock that will be worse than torture for you."* — fol. 7r

The threats serve a dual purpose: they describe **what the technique does** while establishing **why you should fear it**. The violence is not gratuitous — it is the specification. "Force you to the ground" is the functional description; "pain and suffering" is the consequence that motivates the student to learn both the technique and its counter.

**Notable pattern:** Threat intensity escalates within each section. Guards threaten generally ("I will force you to the ground"). First scholars are specific ("I jam his elbow, turn his arm"). Late scholars and counter-masters are decisive ("I will break your arm over my shoulder," "I drive the dagger into his back").

---

## 3. Conditional Tactical Logic (98 passages)

**Where:** Densest in Grappling (12), Dagger masters, Sword in Two Hands — anywhere technique branches.

The most technically valuable mode. Fiore's figures think in decision trees:

> *"And if that lock looks like it will fail me, then I will switch to one of the other locks that follow."* — fol. 6r (Posta Longa)

> *"And if the player who fights with the First Master takes his left hand off the shoulder of the Remedy Master in order to make a defense, then I will quickly let go of his right arm with my left hand and instead seize his left leg."* — fol. 6v

> *"Now I should add that a man may fight at the barrier well armored, with a knowledge of the art of combat, and may have all the advantages possible to have, but if he lacks courage he may as well just hang himself."* — fol. 2r (Preface)

This is where Fiore is most modern. The "if... then... alternatively..." structure maps directly to the way combat sports coaches teach today: primary technique → read the opponent's reaction → follow-up. The conditional structure also encodes the **counter system** — every technique implies its failure mode and the branch to the next option.

**Key insight for grappling history:** This conditional logic is evidence that Fiore's abrazare is not a catalogue of isolated techniques but a **flowchart-based system**. The same structure appears in modern BJJ instruction ("if he defends the armbar, take the triangle").

---

## 4. Philosophical / Reflective (48 passages)

**Where:** Almost entirely in the Preface (16) and Pisani Dossi prefaces (9). Scattered appearances in weapon section introductions.

The solaço/ira distinction is the headline, but Fiore's philosophical voice goes deeper:

### Training vs. Reality

> *"Grappling is of two kinds: one from pleasure, and one from anger — that is, for one's life, with every cunning and deception and cruelty that can be done."* — fol. 2r

> *"Fighting at the barrier is significantly less dangerous than fighting with live swords wearing only padded jackets, because when you fight with live swords a single mistake in your defense could cost you your life."* — fol. 1v

> *"Holds are not superior unless they give you an advantage."* — fol. 6r

### The Art as Intellectual Discipline

> *"This art is so vast that there is no man in the world with so great a memory that he could keep in mind without books a quarter of this art."* — fol. 2r

> *"If I had studied forty years in civil law, in canon law, and in medicine, as I have studied in the art of fencing, then I would be a doctor in those three sciences."* — fol. 2r

> *"Without books no one will ever be a good master nor scholar in this art."* — fol. 2r

### Mortal Stakes

> *"In plays that are taken in compliance, the holds are made with love and do not give ire, whereas in the art of grappling that is made to gain holds, sometimes it is from ire and sometimes for life, and these are holds and plays that cannot be practiced with courtesy, and these are also perilous plays to practice."* — Pisani Dossi, carta 2a

**This is Fiore's thesis for the whole treatise.** He is not teaching a sport. He is documenting a lethal system that he has used in real duels. The "pleasure/anger" distinction is his way of acknowledging that the same movements serve both contexts — but the intent transforms them.

---

## 5. Autobiographical / Credential (33 passages)

**Where:** Preface only (fol. 1r–2r).

Fiore establishes authority through:

**Named students and their victories:**
> *"The first of them was the noble and gallant knight Piero del Verde who fought Piero della Corona. Both were German, and the fight took place in Perosa."*

**Personal duels (five times, with sharp swords):**
> *"This incident — that I was challenged — occurred five times. And five times, for my honor, I was compelled to fight in strange places, without relatives and without friends, having no hope in anything other than in God, in the art, and in me, Fiore, and in my sword."*

**Claim of exclusivity:**
> *"To whom I have taught this art, I have taught secretly, that there was no person other than the scholar and some close relative."*

**The rhetorical strategy:** Fiore front-loads all personal history in the preface, then disappears entirely from the technical sections. The illustrated figures speak — not Fiore. This creates a separation between the author-as-credentialed-expert (preface) and the system-as-self-demonstrating (body). The techniques are meant to be understood on their own terms, not on Fiore's authority.

---

## 6. Counter-Rhetoric (50 passages)

**Where:** Evenly distributed, peaks in Dagger 1st Master (10), Mounted (10), Grappling (5).

The counter-master's voice is the most distinctive rhetorical mode — defiant, dismissive, and structurally embedded:

> *"I make the counter to the play that came before me, so that you will not be able to throw me to the ground, nor take my dagger, nor bind me either."* — Dagger

> *"You will not break my arm over your shoulder, for with my counter I will throw you to the ground."* — Dagger, 1st Master

> *"What you plan to do cannot always be done."* — 2nd Dagger Master

The counter always references the previous technique explicitly ("the play that came before me"), creating a **linked-list structure** in the text. Each counter acknowledges the validity of what it defeats — this is not dismissal but dialectic. The system teaches through opposition.

## Distribution by Manuscript Section

| Section | Dominant Modes | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Preface (1r–4r)** | Autobiographical, Philosophical, Pedagogical | Fiore's personal voice. Earnest, intellectual, defensive about his credentials. |
| **Grappling (6r–8r)** | Threat, Conditional, Boast | Guards boast; scholars threaten and branch. The most "spoken-aloud" section — each figure's voice is distinct. |
| **Dagger (9r–18v)** | Threat, Counter-rhetoric, Conditional | Dense tactical branching. The counter-rhetoric is sharpest here — nine remedy masters, each with counters. |
| **Sword in Two Hands (22v–31v)** | Boast, Pedagogical, Instructional | More formal. Guards declare themselves at length. Plays are more instructional, less dramatic. |
| **Segno (32r)** | Boast (pure) | The four animals speak only in self-declaration. Pure mnemonic. |
| **Sword in Armor (32v–34v)** | Boast, Instructional | Technical and restrained. Armored combat is described more clinically. |
| **Mounted (41r–47r)** | Threat, Counter-rhetoric | Returns to dramatic voice. The horse adds urgency — techniques must work at speed. |

## The Solaço/Ira Framework Across the Manuscript

The "pleasure vs. anger" distinction from fol. 2r is not just a preface remark — it structures the entire manuscript's emotional register:

- **Grappling plays:** The most "ira" — threats of broken arms, dislocations, death. These are the mortal techniques Fiore distinguished from training holds.
- **Guard declarations:** More "solaço" — playful, persona-driven, almost theatrical. The guards are *characters*, not killers.
- **Counter-masters:** The dialectical voice — neither solaço nor ira but **debate**. The counter acknowledges the threat and answers it.
- **Preface:** Uniquely both — Fiore describes his real duels (ira) while reflecting on his art with scholarly detachment (solaço).

This dual register makes Fiore's text unlike any modern martial arts manual. It is simultaneously a technical specification, a dramatic script, and a philosophical treatise on the relationship between practice and violence.
