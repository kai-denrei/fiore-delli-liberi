## Translation

Furlano of Cividale d'Austria [Cividale del Friuli], who was the son of Messer Benedetto of the noble house of the Liberi of Premariacco, of the diocese of the Patriarchate of Aquileia, in his youth wished to learn the art of arms -- the art of fighting in the lists with lance, axe, sword, and dagger, and of wrestling on foot and on horseback, in armour and without armour.

He also wished to learn the tempering of iron.

And the properties of each weapon both for defending and for attacking, and especially those things pertaining to fighting to the finish [a oltranza].

And also other marvellous and extraordinary things which to few men in the world are known.

And these are most true things and of the greatest power, both offensive and defensive, and things that cannot fail, so easy are they to do.

And the said art and mastery as described above.

And the said Fiore learned these said things from many German masters.

And from many Italians in many provinces and in many cities with very great expense.

And by the grace of God, from all masters and scholars.

And he stayed at the courts of great lords, princes, dukes, marquises, and counts, knights and squires, so much did he learn of this art.

That the said Fiore has been requested many times by many lords and knights and squires to teach them this art of arms and of fighting in the lists to the finish, which art he has shown to many Italians and Germans and other great lords who had to fight in the lists.

And also to an infinite number who did not have to fight.

And of some who have been my scholars who had to fight in the lists.

Of whom I shall here name some and make a record.

First of them was the noble and valiant knight Messer Piero del Verde, who had to fight with Messer Piero della Corona, both of whom were Germans.

And the battle was to be at Perugia.

Also to the valorous knight Messer Nicolo de Rizalino, a German, who had to fight with Nicolo, an Englishman. The field was given at Imola.

Also to the notable, valorous, and valiant knight Messer Buleago dei Captani di Grimello, called of Mantua, who had to fight with the valorous knight Messer Boucicaut of France. The field was at Padua.

Also to the valorous squire Lalotto dei Beccaria of Pavia, who had to joust seven points of lance with sharpened tips on horseback against the valiant knight Messer Baldassare, a German, who at Imola had to fight in the lists.

Also to the valorous squire Giovannino da Baggio of Milan, who fought in Pavia in the castle against the valiant squire Siamo, a German, three points of lance with sharpened tips on horseback.

And afterwards on foot three blows of the axe, and three blows of the sword, and three blows of the dagger, in the presence of the most noble prince and lord, my lord the Duke of Milan, and of my lady the Duchess, and other infinite lords and ladies.

Also to the chivalrous knight Messer Azzo di Castel Barco, who had to fight once with Guarneri degli Ordelaffi.

And another time with the valiant and good knight Messer Jacomo de Bosin; the field was to be at the pleasure of the lord Duke of Milan. Of these and others whom I have found or taught, I am very content, because I have been well rewarded and have had the honour and the love of my scholars and their families.

I say also that this art I have always shown secretly so that no one else was present

## Translation

[continuing from fol. 1r] ...at the showing except the scholar and perhaps some discreet relative. And if indeed some other person was there by grace or by courtesy, with oaths they were made to promise in good faith not to reveal anything taught by me, Fiore, master.

And above all I have guarded myself from treacherous masters and from their scholars. And they, out of envy -- these masters have challenged me to fight with sharp swords, both cutting and thrusting, in a padded jacket without other armour save a pair of chamois gloves. And all this has been because I would not associate with them nor would I teach them anything of my art.

And this has happened five times, inasmuch as I have been challenged.

And five times, for my honour, I have been compelled to fight in strange places without relatives and without friends, having no hope in anyone except in God, in my art, in myself, and in my sword.

And by the grace of God, I, Fiore, have survived with honour and without injury to my person.

Furthermore I, Fiore, taught my scholars who had to fight in the barriers that fighting in the barriers is much, much less dangerous than fighting with sharp swords, cutting and thrusting, in a padded jacket. Because he who fights with sharp swords -- if a single cover fails him, that blow gives him death.

But one who fights in the barriers is well armoured and may receive many wounds. He may still win the battle.

Furthermore there is another thing: nine times out of ten nobody dies, because they are taken prisoner.

So I say that I would rather fight three times in the barriers than once with sharp swords as said above.

And I say that the way to fight in the barriers, being well armed, and knowing the art of fighting, and having the advantages that one can take -- if he is not valiant he would wish to spit [with disgust]. But I can say by the grace of God that never has any of my scholars been the loser. All of them have always remained with honour.

Furthermore I, the aforesaid Fiore, say that these lords, knights, and squires to whom I showed this art of fighting have been content with my teaching, wanting no one else but me as their master.

Furthermore I say that none of my scholars, especially the aforesaid, ever had a book on the art of fighting except Messer Galeazzo of Mantua.

Although he proves that without books no one will ever make a good master or scholar in this art.

And I, Fiore, confirm this to be true, that this art is so vast that there is no man in the world with so great a memory that he could keep in mind without books a quarter of this art.

Therefore with only a quarter of this art, knowing no more, I would not consider myself a master.

Because I, Fiore, knowing how to read, and write, and draw, and having books on this art, and in it having studied a good forty years or more -- still I do not feel myself perfectly accomplished in all of this art. Although I am held by the great lords who have been my scholars to be a perfectly accomplished master in the aforesaid art.

And I say that if I had studied forty years in law, in canon law, and in medicine as I have studied in the art of arms, I would be a doctor in those three sciences.

And in this science of arms I have had great toil with study and the presumption of being merely a scholar of someone else.

Considering -- I the aforesaid Fiore -- that in this art few in the world are found who are masters, and wishing that a memorial be made of me, therefore I shall make a book of all the art, and of all things which I know, of iron and of tempering and of other things, according to the order which has been given to me by that high Lord who above all others for martial virtue pleased me most, and most deserving of this my book for his nobility than any other lord whom I have ever seen or shall be able to see, that is my illustrious and exalted lord, powerful prince...

[Illuminated Q:] ...Marquis of Este, Lord of the noble city of Ferrara, of Modena, Reggio, and Parma, to whom may God give good life, and prosperous fortune, and victory over his enemies.

We begin the book according to the instruction of my lord the Marquis, and we shall ensure that nothing is lacking in the art, for I am certain that my lord will give me good reward for his great nobility and courtesy.

And we begin with wrestling. Which is of two kinds: one of play [solazo] and one of anger [ira], that is for one's life with every cunning and deception and cruelty that can be done. And of those things that are done for one's life I wish first to show by reason, and especially how to gain the holds, as in earnest when one fights for one's life.

The man who wishes to wrestle must be aware of whom he wrestles with. Whether his companion is stronger, or whether he is larger of person. And whether he is too young or too old. Also to see if he puts himself in the guards of wrestling. And all these things must be considered.

And nonetheless, always set oneself -- whether stronger or weaker -- to the holds and the locks. And always defend oneself from the holds of one's adversary.

And if your enemy is unarmed, aim to strike him in the most painful and most dangerous places: that is in the eyes, and the nose, and the temples, and under the chin, and in the flanks.

And nonetheless see if you can come to holds and locks, whether armed or unarmed, whichever one and the other may be.

Furthermore I say that wrestling requires eight things. That is: strength, speed, knowledge...

## Notes
- This folio continues from fol. 1r (mid-sentence) and concludes the autobiographical prologue.
- Key passage: Fiore's five personal duels with sharp swords in a padded jacket (*çuparello d'armare*) and chamois gloves.
- The illuminated initial "Q" introduces the dedication to Niccolo III d'Este, Marquis of Ferrara (the patron who commissioned this manuscript).
- The critical *solazo/ira* distinction begins at the bottom of the right column -- wrestling "for play" vs. wrestling "for one's life."
- The eight attributes (*viij. chose*) of wrestling are introduced at the very end but the list is cut off, continuing on fol. 4r.
- Corrections from existing OCR: "solaço" corrected to "solaço" (confirmed from image), "plazo" corrected to "solaço", various abbreviation expansions refined.
- Text breaks mid-list at the bottom of the right column.

Also knowing how to dislocate arms and legs in various ways.

The guards of wrestling can be made in various ways. And one way is better than another.
